In "Robinson Crusoe," Daniel Defoe explores themes of survival, resilience, and the human condition. Through the protagonist's journey of self-discovery and adaptation to life on a deserted island, Defoe delves into the complexities of human nature, the struggle for existence, and the quest for meaning in adversity. Additionally, the novel reflects societal and philosophical ideas of the time, such as the Protestant work ethic, the Enlightenment ideals of rationality and progress, and the exploration of colonialism and imperialism. Ultimately, "Robinson Crusoe" serves as a timeless exploration of the human spirit and its capacity to endure and thrive in the face of isolation and hardship.
